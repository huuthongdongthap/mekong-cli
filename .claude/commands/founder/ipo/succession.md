# /founder buyback — Share Buyback Intelligence

## USAGE
```
/founder buyback [--model <amount> | --announce | --execute]
```

## WHEN BUYBACKS MAKE SENSE

```
BUYBACK = company uses cash to repurchase own shares
Effect: fewer shares outstanding → EPS goes up → stock goes up

SIGNALS:
  Good signal: "We believe stock is undervalued"
  Bad signal:  "We have no better uses for cash"
  
WHEN TO BUYBACK:
  □ Cash > 2 years of operating expenses (excess cash)
  □ Stock is trading below intrinsic value (your view)
  □ No better investment returns available (no good M&A targets)
  □ EPS accretion > cost of capital

WHEN NOT TO:
  ✗ You're still growing fast (invest in growth instead)
  ✗ Stock near all-time high (expensive buyback)
  ✗ Cash needed for operations
  ✗ Better acquisition targets exist

MECHANICS:
  Board approves buyback program: "$500M over 24 months"
  Company executes via open market or 10b5-1 plan
  ASR: Accelerated Share Repurchase (large block, faster)
  
  Announce via 8-K:
  "Board of Directors approved $X million share repurchase
   program. Company may repurchase shares from time to time
   in the open market or through private transactions."

BUYBACK ACCRETION MODEL:
  Shares outstanding: 100M
  Buyback: $100M at $20/share = 5M shares
  New shares outstanding: 95M
  
  If earnings flat at $100M:
    Before: $100M / 100M = $1.00 EPS
    After:  $100M / 95M = $1.05 EPS (+5%)
  
  Multiple expansion if P/E stays same:
    $1.05 × 20x P/E = $21 stock price (vs $20 before)
```

# /founder m-and-a — Post-IPO M&A Engine

## USAGE
```
/founder m-and-a [--screen | --model <target> | --integrate]
```

## ACQUIRE FROM STRENGTH

```
WHY PUBLIC COMPANIES ACQUIRE:
  □ Buy revenue growth (faster than organic)
  □ Buy technology (faster than building)
  □ Buy customers (eliminate competitor + get their users)
  □ Buy talent (acqui-hire)
  □ Buy market share (consolidation)
  □ Buy distribution (new channels)

YOUR NEW WEAPON: PUBLIC COMPANY STOCK
  Before IPO: you paid cash or gave expensive private equity
  After IPO: you can use stock as currency
  
  If stock is at premium multiple:
    Acquire with stock = cheap acquisition relative to earnings
    "Stock-financed acquisition" — no cash needed
    
  Facebook buying Instagram for $1B in 2012:
    Used stock when FB multiple was sky-high
    "Buying" Instagram with overvalued currency = cheap in hindsight

SCREENING CRITERIA:
  □ Target size: 5-30% of your market cap (manageable)
  □ Revenue multiple: cheaper than yours = accretive
  □ Strategic fit: product adjacency or customer overlap
  □ Technology: fills gap faster than building
  □ Team quality: key people must stay post-acquisition
  □ Integration risk: lower is better

M&A PROCESS:
  Week 1-2:   Identify + initial screen
  Week 3-4:   Approach + NDA
  Week 5-8:   Due diligence
  Week 9-10:  Negotiation + term sheet
  Week 11-14: Definitive agreement
  Week 15-20: Regulatory approval
  Month 5-6:  Close + integration

INTEGRATION PLAYBOOK:
  Day 1:   All-hands with acquired team (CEO)
  Week 1:  Systems access + HR onboarding
  Month 1: Product integration plan finalized
  Month 3: Revenue synergies beginning
  Month 6: Technology integration checkpoint
  Year 1:  Full integration complete
  
  Rule: Every day of uncertain integration = talent walks out
```

# /founder succession — Leadership Succession Planning

## USAGE
```
/founder succession [--plan | --emergency | --transition]
```

## WHY FOUNDER PLANS SUCCESSION

```
"Planning succession doesn't mean you're leaving.
 It means the company is bigger than any one person."
 — Every good board member you'll ever meet

WHY BOARDS ASK FOR SUCCESSION PLAN:
  □ Institutional investors require it
  □ Risk mitigation (what if you're hit by a bus?)
  □ Shows organizational maturity
  □ Required for Governance scores (ESG/proxy advisors)
  □ Prevents hostile takeover when transition needed

TYPES OF SUCCESSION:
  Emergency: CEO incapacitated unexpectedly
  Planned: CEO steps to Chairman, new CEO hired
  Founder-to-CEO: founder becomes Executive Chairman
  Full exit: founder leaves company entirely
```

## SUCCESSION PLAN FRAMEWORK

```
FILE: .mekong/ipo/succession-plan.md (CONFIDENTIAL)

EMERGENCY SUCCESSION:
  Immediate successor if CEO unavailable:
    Name: {COO or CFO}
    Scope: operational decisions for up to 90 days
    Limitations: no major acquisitions, no fundraising
    
  Board notification protocol:
    Who calls whom, in what order
    Communications plan for employees, investors, press

PLANNED SUCCESSION SCENARIOS:

  SCENARIO A — Founder stays as CEO:
    (most common, most preferred by investors)
    Signs of success: company scaling well under founder
    
    WHAT TO BUILD:
    □ Strong COO who runs operations
    □ CFO with public company expertise
    □ Board that advises, doesn't micromanage
    □ Succession bench: who could be CEO in 5 years?

  SCENARIO B — Founder → Executive Chairman:
    When: Company needs different CEO skillset for scale
    Founder retains: board control, strategy, culture
    New CEO: operational excellence, sales motion
    
    Successful examples:
      Travis Kalanick → Dara Khosrowshahi (Uber)
      Jack Dorsey → Parag Agrawal (Twitter)
      
    Keys to success:
    □ Founder must genuinely support new CEO
    □ Clear division of responsibilities (confusion = failure)
    □ Board ensures new CEO gets real authority

  SCENARIO C — Full Transition:
    Founder exits completely
    Rare for IPO-stage companies
    Only with: full liquidity, philosophical disagreement

CEO CANDIDATE PROFILE (internal):
  Characteristics needed for NEXT phase:
    □ Public company experience (earnings, Reg FD)
    □ {specific skill your company needs to scale}
    □ Cultural fit (can they sustain what you built?)
    □ Board management experience
    □ Global/operational scale experience

BOARD'S ROLE IN SUCCESSION:
  □ Annual CEO performance review
  □ Succession committee (3 independent directors)
  □ Annual update: "Who could be CEO in 12 months?"
  □ External search readiness (always have a short list)
```
