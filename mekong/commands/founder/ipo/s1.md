### SECTION 1: PROSPECTUS SUMMARY

```
THE HOOK (1-2 paragraphs — most read section):

Formula:
  [Market transformation happening] + [Why now] + [Our position] +
  [Traction proof] + [What this IPO enables]

DRAFT TEMPLATE:
  "{Industry} is undergoing its most fundamental transformation
   since {reference point}. {Market size}+ businesses now {trend}.
   
   {Company} was built for this moment. We {what we do} for
   {who} — enabling them to {outcome}. Since {founding},
   we've grown to {ARR} ARR, serving {n} customers in {n} countries,
   with {NRR}% net revenue retention.
   
   This offering will accelerate our mission to {big vision},
   a {TAM} global market opportunity."

FINANCIAL HIGHLIGHTS BOX:
  As of {date}:
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Revenue:          ${n}M ({growth}% YoY)│
  │  ARR:              ${n}M                 │
  │  Gross Margin:     {pct}%               │
  │  Customers:        {n}                  │
  │  Net Revenue Ret:  {pct}%               │
  │  Rule of 40:       {score}             │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### SECTION 2: BUSINESS DESCRIPTION

```
THE PROBLEM (make investors feel the pain):
  Quantify: "Companies spend $X billion on {problem}"
  Humanize: "Today, {persona} spends {n} hours on {task}"
  Status quo failure: "Existing solutions fail because..."

OUR SOLUTION:
  Core product description (non-technical)
  Key differentiators (3-5, must be defensible)
  How we make money (clear, simple)

MARKET OPPORTUNITY:
  TAM: Total Addressable Market (go big but credible)
  SAM: Serviceable Addressable Market
  SOM: Current target

  TAM CALCULATION APPROACHES:
    Top-down: industry report × your relevant slice
    Bottom-up: customers × ACV × market penetration
    Both: show both, they should converge

  Example framing:
    "We estimate our TAM at $X billion, representing
     {methodology}. Our current SAM — {segment} —
     represents $Y billion, of which we've captured {pct}%."

COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE:
  Position matrix (NEVER say "no competition"):
  
  Axis options: price vs features, speed vs quality, etc.
  Show: where you uniquely win
  
  Key competitors section:
    Legacy players: "Built for the previous era"
    Point solutions: "Solve one piece, not the system"
    Internal builds: "We replace DIY with enterprise-grade"
  
  Why we win:
    □ Network effects (if any)
    □ Data moat (if any)
    □ Switching costs (if any)
    □ Technology lead (if any)

GROWTH STRATEGY:
  Land and expand: how we grow within accounts
  Geographic expansion: market by market
  Product expansion: new products for existing customers
  New segments: adjacent markets
```

### SECTION 3: KEY METRICS

```
NORTH STAR METRIC:
  Define it. Explain why it matters.
  Show 12 quarters of growth.
  Explain any inflection points.

COHORT ANALYSIS (most important for SaaS):
  Revenue retention by cohort
  "Every cohort we've acquired has grown over time"
  = moat = justifies premium multiple

UNIT ECONOMICS:
  CAC: "We acquire customers at $X"
  LTV: "Each customer generates $Y over Z months"
  LTV:CAC: "Every dollar we invest returns $X"
  Payback period: "{n} months" (< 12 = great)

RULE OF 40:
  Revenue growth % + FCF margin %
  > 40 = healthy growth company
  > 60 = exceptional

METRICS TABLE (quarterly, 8-12 quarters):
  Quarter  Revenue  ARR     NRR    Customers  GM%   Rule40
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Q1'23    $XM      $YM     110%   N          65%   45
  Q2'23    ...

WHAT NOT TO INCLUDE:
  ❌ Vanity metrics (downloads, signups without conversion)
  ❌ Metrics that peaked and declined
  ❌ Metrics you can't defend in Q&A
  ✓ Only include metrics that tell your story of GROWTH + QUALITY
```

### SECTION 4: RISK FACTORS

**Agent: CFO / sonnet / careful drafting / 5 MCU**

```
PHILOSOPHY: Risk factors protect you legally.
            Disclose everything material. Investors expect risks.
            Surprising investors post-IPO = lawsuits.

CATEGORIES:

1. BUSINESS RISKS:
   □ Competition from larger companies with more resources
   □ Dependence on key customers (if concentration exists)
   □ Product liability / service failures
   □ International expansion risks
   □ Technology changes making product obsolete

2. FINANCIAL RISKS:
   □ History of net losses (if applicable)
   □ May continue to incur losses
   □ Revenue concentration
   □ Foreign currency exposure

3. OPERATIONAL RISKS:
   □ Key personnel dependence (founder!)
   □ Ability to hire and retain talent
   □ System reliability / cybersecurity
   □ Third-party dependencies (AWS, Anthropic API, etc.)

4. REGULATORY RISKS:
   □ AI regulation (increasingly important)
   □ Data privacy laws evolving
   □ International regulations vary

5. FOUNDER-SPECIFIC (disclose honestly):
   □ Founder holds supervoting shares (if applicable)
   □ Founder may have competing interests
   □ Loss of key founder = material adverse effect

DRAFTING RULE: Write risks completely and honestly.
Understating risks = securities fraud.
Overstating risks = nobody notices.
```

### SECTION 5: USE OF PROCEEDS

```
Where will IPO money go? (Investors care.)

TEMPLATE:
  "We estimate we will receive net proceeds of approximately
   $X million from this offering.
   
   We intend to use the proceeds as follows:
   • Product development: $Xm (XX%)
   • Sales and marketing: $Xm (XX%)  
   • International expansion: $Xm (XX%)
   • General corporate: $Xm (XX%)
   • Working capital: remainder
   
   We have no current plans for significant acquisitions."

AVOID: "General corporate purposes" only = no vision
```

### SECTION 6: MANAGEMENT + GOVERNANCE

```
CEO/FOUNDER BIO:
  3-4 sentences. Emphasize:
  □ Relevant domain expertise
  □ Previous success (if any)
  □ Why you specifically = right person for this

EXECUTIVE TEAM:
  Each exec: title + prior company + specific achievement

BOARD OF DIRECTORS:
  Independent directors: why they add value
  Audit committee: who qualifies as "financial expert"

DUAL-CLASS SHARES (protect founder control):
  Structure: Class A (1 vote) public, Class B (10 votes) founder
  Precedent: Google, Facebook, Snap, Lyft all did this
  
  S-1 language:
  "Following this offering, holders of Class B common stock
   will hold approximately XX% of the voting power.
   {Founder names} will control the outcome of matters
   submitted to stockholders for approval."
  
  ⚠️  Some institutional investors oppose dual-class.
      ISS (proxy advisor) often recommends against.
      But Founder Vision justified if track record strong.
```

## NARRATIVE QUALITY CHECKLIST

```
Before filing, every section must pass:
  □ "So what?" test — does every fact connect to investor value?
  □ "Can they remember this?" — is there a sticky narrative?
  □ "Will analysts quote this?" — are there quotable data points?
  □ "Is it internally consistent?" — do all sections align?
  □ "Would a first-year analyst understand it?" — clarity test
  □ "Does the CEO believe every word?" — authenticity test
```
